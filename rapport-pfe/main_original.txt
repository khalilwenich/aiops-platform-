Abstract

This final year project, carried out at Capgemini -- Altran Telnet Corpora-
tion Tunisia, consists of designing and developing an AIOps platform (Artificial
Intelligence for IT Operations) for the automated analysis of CI/CD pipelines.

    The solution receives GitLab events in real time through secure webhooks, collects
quality (SonarQube) and security (Trivy) reports, then submits the aggregated context
to a Large Language Model (Groq -- Llama 3.3 70B) to produce a root-cause analysis,
a risk score and prioritized fix suggestions -- all within ten seconds. The platform also
provides incident management, a self-feeding knowledge base, a per-project health score
(grade A to F) and weekly aggregated reports, presented in a real-time interactive React
dashboard. It is containerized with Docker, deployed on Kubernetes (k3s) through
Helm, and equipped with a complete Jenkins CI/CD pipeline featuring automated tests.

    Keywords: AIOps, CI/CD, Root-Cause Analysis, LLM, GitLab, Kubernetes,
Jenkins, Node.js, React.

General Introduction

Modern software engineering relies heavily on DevOps methodologies, which bring
development and operations teams closer together to deliver faster and more reliably. At
the heart of this approach, Continuous Integration and Continuous Deployment (CI/CD)
pipelines automate the building, testing and release of software. This automation,
however, has a downside: it produces a considerable amount of technical data --
execution logs, code-quality reports, vulnerability scans -- whose manual exploitation
quickly becomes unmanageable.

    When a pipeline fails, the engineer must scan thousands of log lines, cross-reference
the results of several heterogeneous tools and identify the root cause alone. This
diagnosis takes, on average, 30 to 90 minutes per incident, often for recurring problems
that were already solved in the past but never capitalized.

    This is the context of the present final year project, which proposes an AIOps
platform applying artificial intelligence to the supervision of CI/CD pipelines. The
goal is to automate analysis, centralize data sources, provide AI-assisted root-cause
diagnosis, and capitalize recurring solutions.

    This report is organized into seven chapters. The first chapter presents the general
context, the host organization, the study of the existing situation and the proposed
solution. The second chapter surveys the state of the art and positions the project's
contribution relative to existing commercial and academic work. The third chapter
covers the analysis and specification of functional and non-functional requirements.
The fourth chapter details the design of the solution, its architecture, data models,
services and APIs. The fifth chapter describes the implementation, testing, deployment
(Docker, Helm, Kubernetes, Jenkins) and the results obtained. The sixth chapter
critically discusses the limitations of the solution and the mitigations identified. Finally,
the seventh chapter covers the project-management aspects (work breakdown, risk
register), before the general conclusion, future work and annexes.

1.2 Host Organization

This project was carried out at Capgemini -- Altran Telnet Corporation (ATC)
Tunisia. ATC is a joint venture created in 2008 between the Altran group and
the Tunisian group Telnet Holding. The company specializes in engineering and the
development of embedded software and real-time systems, notably for the defense,
security, aeronautics and automotive sectors.

    Following the acquisition of Altran by the Capgemini group in 2020, the entity
progressively joined the Capgemini Engineering brand, the business line dedicated
to engineering and R&D services within the Capgemini group. Capgemini is today a
global leader in consulting, technology services and digital transformation, present in
more than 50 countries. Locally, ATC supports several international client companies
in their DevOps and platform-engineering transformation, where engineering teams
face failing CI/CD pipelines on a daily basis -- precisely the operational pain point
addressed by this project.

1.3 Study of the Existing Situation

In current practice, diagnosing a failed pipeline relies on an entirely manual process.
The engineer must:
· scan thousands of CI log lines manually;
· cross-reference the results of several independent tools (GitLab CI, SonarQube,

    Trivy) in separate interfaces;
· identify the root cause without any automated assistance;
· manage incidents without centralized traceability;
· waste time on recurring problems whose solutions are not documented.
Furthermore, several tools exist on the market but remain siloed: GitLab CI provides
logs, SonarQube analyzes code quality, Trivy detects image vulnerabilities, while classic
observability stacks (Prometheus, Grafana, ELK) focus on infrastructure metrics rather

                                                       5
Chapter 1. General Context and Problem Statement  AIOps Platform

than pipeline-level root-cause diagnosis. Commercial APM/AIOps platforms (Datadog,
Dynatrace) offer advanced capabilities but remain costly, generic, and not tailored to
CI/CD-specific failure analysis.

1.4 Critique of the Existing Situation

The analysis highlights the following limitations:

· Scattered data that is never correlated across sources;

· No automated, contextualized root-cause analysis;

· No memorization of solutions to recurring problems;

· Alerting without diagnosis, which reports failures without explaining their origin;

· High diagnosis time (30 to 90 minutes) and poor incident traceability.

2.1 Introduction

AIOps is an active commercial and research field. This chapter surveys the main
categories of existing solutions -- commercial AIOps/APM suites, CI/CD-native tooling,
and academic work on log-based root-cause analysis -- and positions the contribution
of this project relative to each.

2.2 Commercial AIOps and Observability Platforms

2.2.1 Datadog and Dynatrace

Datadog and Dynatrace are the two dominant commercial Application Performance
Monitoring (APM) suites with AIOps modules (Datadog Watchdog, Dynatrace Davis
AI). Both excel at infrastructure and application-level anomaly detection (latency spikes,
error-rate deviations) using statistical and ML models trained on metrics and traces.
However, neither is designed around CI/CD pipeline semantics: they do not natively
parse GitLab/GitHub CI job logs, do not cross-reference a SonarQube quality gate with
a Trivy CVE scan for the same commit, and their root-cause narratives are generated
from telemetry correlation rather than from a generative-AI reading of the actual failure
context. They are also priced per host/container ingested, which makes them costly for
a use case centered on CI pipelines rather than production infrastructure.

2.2.2 Moogsoft and BigPanda

Moogsoft and BigPanda focus on alert correlation: deduplicating and clustering thou-
sands of monitoring alerts into a smaller number of actionable incidents. This is
conceptually close to the correlation service implemented in this project, but again
operates at the infrastructure-alert level, not at the CI/CD pipeline-failure level, and
does not produce a natural-language root-cause explanation or a concrete code-level fix

                                                      10
Chapter 2. State of the Art and Comparative Study  AIOps Platform

suggestion.

2.2.3 PagerDuty / Jeli and incident-management tools

PagerDuty (and its acquisition Jeli) provide mature incident lifecycle management with
timelines, on-call escalation and post-incident reviews -- directly comparable to the
Incident model and post-mortem feature of this project. Their post-mortems, however,
remain manually authored templates; they do not auto-generate a draft post-mortem
from an LLM reading the incident timeline and the associated technical root cause,
which is what this project automates.

2.3 CI/CD-Native Tooling

GitLab CI, SonarQube and Trivy -- the three data sources integrated by this platform
-- each solve one slice of the problem in isolation: GitLab CI executes and logs the
pipeline, SonarQube evaluates static code quality, and Trivy scans container images
for known CVEs. None of the three correlates its own output with the other two, and
none performs root-cause reasoning over a failure; they report what happened, never
why, nor what to do about it. GitHub's own "Copilot for pipelines" / GitHub Actions
failure summaries (announced in 2024) point in the same direction as this project but
remain tied to the GitHub ecosystem and, at the time of writing, only summarize a
single job log without cross-tool correlation or a persisted, searchable knowledge base.

2.4 Academic Background: LLMs for Log Analysis

       and RCA

Recent academic work (e.g. studies on LogGPT-style log-anomaly detection, and on
using LLMs for incident root-cause summarization in large-scale cloud operations [1])
confirms two findings directly relevant to this project's design: (1) general-purpose
LLMs, when given well-structured context (relevant log excerpts rather than entire
raw logs), perform competitively with specialized log-parsing models on root-cause
classification, which justifies the normalization/correlation step before the LLM call
rather than feeding raw logs directly; and (2) caching/memorizing previously diagnosed
patterns substantially reduces both cost and latency for recurring failures -- the exact
rationale behind this project's knowledge-base service.

3.1 Introduction

This chapter identifies the platform actors, lists the functional and non-functional
requirements, and formalizes them through use-case modeling.

3.2 Actor Identification

The platform relies on Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) with three levels:
· Administrator: all operations (deletion, recomputation, configuration);
· Analyst: analyses, resolution, comments and post-mortems;
· Viewer: read-only access.
A system actor is also involved: the GitLab server, which emits pipeline and merge-
request events through webhooks.

3.3 Functional Requirements

3.3.1 Dashboard

The dashboard is the system's landing page and must give an engineer, within seconds
of logging in, a synthetic view of the state of all supervised projects: the list of recent
pipelines with their status, the global success rate over the last seven days plotted as a
trend chart, a small set of key performance indicators (active incidents, average MTTR,
open critical vulnerabilities), a ranked list of the top recurring issues across projects,
and a live feed of notifications pushed over WebSocket as new analyses complete. The
dashboard must remain usable with a global project filter, since an engineer typically
cares about a subset of the supervised projects rather than the entire fleet.

                                                      14
Chapter 3. Requirements Analysis and Specification  AIOps Platform

3.3.2 Pipeline management

The system must expose a paginated, filterable list of pipelines (filterable by status,
branch, project and date range), since the number of pipelines grows continuously and
an unbounded list would not scale. Selecting a pipeline must reveal its full detail: the
list of jobs and their individual status, the raw logs of the jobs that failed, and -- when
available -- the associated AI analysis. An analyst must also be able to manually
re-trigger the analysis of a given pipeline, for instance after fixing the underlying tool
configuration and wanting to confirm the diagnosis was accurate.

3.3.3 AI analysis -- Root-Cause Analysis

For every analyzed pipeline, the system must classify the failure into one of a fixed
set of error types (build failure, test failure, dependency issue, security vulnerability,
configuration error, or unknown), produce a natural-language explanation of the root
cause, assign a risk level (critical/high/medium/low) together with a numeric confidence
score, and propose one or more severity-prioritized fix suggestions, each optionally
carrying a ready-to-run shell command or a code hint. The system must also list the
files it identified as affected, and must detect when a failure matches a previously seen
pattern so the recurring-issue view (Section 3.3.1, Dashboard) can surface it.

3.3.4 Security and code quality

The system must integrate Trivy's vulnerability scan results, exposing severity counters
(critical/high/medium/low), the full list of detected CVEs with package and fixed-
version information, and a workflow to mark a given vulnerability as acknowledged or
ignored. It must equally integrate SonarQube metrics -- quality-gate status, bug count,
vulnerability count, code-smell count and test coverage -- since these feed directly into
the health-score computation (Section 4.6.1) and into the AI analysis context.

3.3.5 Knowledge base

Whenever an analyst resolves an analysis, the system must automatically memorize
the validated solution under a unique signature derived from the error type and root
cause, so that a future occurrence of the same error is answered instantly from the
cache rather than triggering a new, costlier LLM call. The knowledge base must also be
independently browsable and searchable (full-text, across title, tags, error type and root
cause) so an analyst can proactively consult it before even waiting for an automated
analysis.

15
Chapter 3. Requirements Analysis and Specification  AIOps Platform

3.3.6 Health score, incidents and reports

The system must continuously compute, per project, a composite health score between
0 and 100, mapped to a letter grade from A to F, and must expose its full breakdown
(the six weighted metrics of Table 4.3) rather than the score alone, so that an engineer
understands why a project is graded the way it is. Failed pipelines must automatically
open an incident, which the system tracks through a lifecycle (open  investigating 
resolved) with a timestamped timeline, an MTTR computation, and an on-demand AI-
generated post-mortem once resolved. Open merge requests must receive an automatic
risk comment summarizing the latest analysis. Finally, the system must produce a
weekly aggregated report per project, comparable week-over-week, summarizing pipeline
volume, success rate, new vulnerabilities and incident count.

3.4 Non-Functional Requirements

· Security: JWT authentication, three-level RBAC, HMAC-SHA256 webhook valida-
    tion, Bcrypt hashing, HTTP security headers.

· Performance: pipeline analysis in under ten seconds.
· Real time: dashboard updates over WebSocket.
· Scalability: asynchronous processing via message queues and concurrent workers.
· Reliability: error recovery with retries and exponential backoff.
· Maintainability: layered architecture, modular and documented code.
· Portability: full containerization and reproducible Kubernetes deployment.

4.6.2 Knowledge-base service

This service stores solutions to recurring errors. Each entry is keyed by a determin-
istic signature MD5(errorType + ":" + rootCause[:100]), normalized to lowercase.
When an analyst marks an analysis as resolved, the solution is memorized; on a later
similar analysis, a cache hit injects the known solution at the top of the suggested fixes,
avoiding a redundant LLM call.

4.7.1 API design rationale

Three design decisions shape every endpoint of the API. First, pagination is manda-
tory on every list endpoint (?page=, ?limit=, capped at 100) to avoid unbounded
MongoDB scans as the number of pipelines grows over time. Second, errors follow a
single envelope { error: { code, message } } with a stable machine-readable
code (e.g. INVALID_SIGNATURE, NOT_FOUND, FORBIDDEN), so that the frontend can
branch on code rather than parsing free-text messages. Third, the webhook ingestion
endpoint deliberately returns 202 Accepted rather than 200/201: the HTTP response

                                                      33
Chapter 4. Design and Architecture      AIOps Platform

only confirms that the event was queued, not that the analysis is complete, which keeps
GitLab's webhook delivery (which times out after 10 s) decoupled from the LLM call
latency.

4.7.2 Database design rationale

MongoDB was chosen over a relational database for three reasons specific to this domain.
First, the Analysis document's shape varies with the error type (a security_vulnerability
analysis carries CVE references that a build_failure analysis never has); a flexible
schema avoids a wide table with mostly-null columns or a fragile EAV (Entity-Attribute-
Value) relational pattern. Second, the embedded sub-documents identified in Figure 4.5
(suggestedFixes, timeline, breakdown) are always read together with their parent
and never queried independently, which is the textbook case for document embedding
versus normalization. Third, the write pattern is append-heavy and time-ordered (new
pipelines, new analyses), which plays to MongoDB's strengths with capped growth and
TTL-friendly collections, should historical pruning be needed later.

    Indexing follows the query patterns actually issued by the dashboard: Pipeline is
indexed on (projectId, createdAt) for the per-project timeline and on pipelineId
for webhook lookups; Analysis is indexed on pipelineId (unique lookup from the
pipeline detail page) and on errorType (recurring-issues aggregation); KnowledgeBase
is indexed on the unique errorSignature field, since every cache lookup is an exact-
match query on that field; ProjectHealth is indexed on the unique projectId, since
only the latest score per project is read on the dashboard.

4.9 Frontend Architecture

The frontend is a React (Vite) single-page application. Global state is split between
Redux Toolkit (authentication tokens, active filters), TanStack Query (server-
state cache, queries and mutations with cache invalidation) and a Socket.io client
for real-time events. Received events such as analysis:complete, pipeline:new and
incident:created invalidate the relevant queries, refreshing the dashboard instantly.
The application is structured into eleven pages (Dashboard, Pipelines, Analyses, Security,
Health Score, Incidents, Knowledge Base, Weekly Report, Settings, etc.) and reusable
component families (layout, dashboard, analysis, health, incident, knowledge, report,
security, generic UI).

Mitigations

6.1 Introduction

A defensible final-year project should not only present what works, but also reason
explicitly about what does not yet, and why. This chapter anticipates the questions a
review jury is most likely to raise about the platform described in Chapters 3 and 4,
organized by category, together with the mitigation already in place or planned.

6.2 Methodological Limitations

6.2.1 AI classification accuracy measured on a limited sample

The 85% error-type classification accuracy reported in Table 5.8 was measured on a few
dozen real pipeline failures observed during the project's sprints, not on a statistically
representative, labeled benchmark. This is a methodological weakness: the figure
should be read as an encouraging early signal, not a validated production SLA. A
rigorous follow-up would build a labeled dataset of at least a few hundred failures
across multiple projects and report precision/recall per errorType rather than a single
aggregate accuracy.

6.2.2 No formal usability study

The dashboard's usability was validated informally (the author's own use, and brief
feedback from the host team) rather than through a structured usability test with
target engineers. A System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire administered to the
DevOps unit would strengthen the claim that the tool measurably reduces cognitive
load, beyond the timing measurements of Table 1.3.

6.3 Architectural Limitations

                                                      54
Chapter 6. Critical Discussion, Limitations and Mitigations  AIOps Platform

6.3.1 Single replica, no high availability

Every Deployment in the current Helm chart (Table 5.1, Section 5.3) runs a single
replica. The backend is, however, designed stateless by construction -- session state
lives in JWTs, not in process memory, and job state lives in Redis/BullMQ -- so
horizontal scaling to replicas: N is a one-line values.yaml change with no code
modification, as already verified manually (Table 3.1, scalability row). What is genuinely
missing is a HorizontalPodAutoscaler and a load balancer health-check tuned for the
/api/health endpoint, both straightforward additions deferred for time reasons.

6.3.2 Single MongoDB instance, no replica set or backup policy

MongoDB runs as a single pod with a single PVC and no replica set, meaning a
node failure or PVC corruption would cause data loss. The mitigation path is well
understood (a 3-node replica set via the Bitnami MongoDB Helm chart, plus a scheduled
mongodump CronJob to object storage) but was not implemented within the project's
time budget, since the lab cluster has a single VM/node and a replica set's benefit is
mostly demonstrative in that topology.

6.3.3 Secrets management without a vault

Sensitive values (JWT secret, GitLab token, LLM API keys) are kept in a git-ignored
values.secrets.yaml injected as Kubernetes Opaque secrets (base64-encoded, not
encrypted at rest by default in etcd). A production deployment should instead use
a secret manager (HashiCorp Vault, or Sealed Secrets / SOPS for a GitOps-friendly
alternative) so that secrets are encrypted both at rest and in the Git history of the
deployment repository, if one were used.

6.4 Data Privacy and Third-Party LLM Depen-
       dency

Every LLM call sends a context excerpt (log lines, commit messages, SonarQube issue
descriptions) to a third-party API (Groq, with Claude as backup). For the lab projects
used during this PFE this is an acceptable trade-off, but it would not be acceptable
as-is for a client project under strict data-residency or confidentiality clauses. Three
mitigations are already partially in place or planned: (1) the correlation/normalization
step (Section 4.4) only forwards the relevant excerpt of a failing job's log, not the entire
raw log, which already limits exposure; (2) the knowledge-base cache (Section 4.6.2)
means a recurring error is diagnosed by the LLM only once, after which the memorized

55
Chapter 6. Critical Discussion, Limitations and Mitigations  AIOps Platform

solution is reused without any further data leaving the platform; (3) the dual-provider
design (Groq primary, Claude backup) could be extended with a third, self-hosted
option (e.g. a quantized Llama model served via Ollama inside the cluster) for clients
who require on-premises inference, at the cost of classification quality -- a trade-off to
be validated, not assumed.

6.5 Operational and Process Limitations

· Manual CI/CD trigger: the Jenkins pipeline is currently started via "Build Now"
    rather than an automatic GitHub webhook, because the lab Jenkins instance is not
    exposed on a public endpoint reachable from GitHub. The Jenkinsfile and pipeline
    logic are already webhook-ready; only the trigger configuration is missing.

· Coarse-grained RBAC: the three roles (admin/analyst/viewer) are global, not
    scoped per project. A client onboarding several teams with different project visibility
    would need project-level permissions, not implemented in the current model.

· Limited automated test coverage: as discussed in Section 5.6.2, only the unit
    layer of the test pyramid is automated; integration and end-to-end tests are identified
    but not yet implemented.

· No production monitoring of the platform itself : the AIOps platform monitors
   client CI/CD pipelines, but its own health (API latency, worker queue depth, LLM
    error rate) is not yet exported to Prometheus/Grafana, which is an ironic but honest
    gap for an observability-oriented tool.

The AIOps platform developed in this final year project addresses a concrete problem
faced daily by the DevOps teams at Capgemini -- Altran Telnet Corporation Tunisia.

system: automatic root-cause analysis in under ten seconds, organizational memory
through the self-feeding knowledge base, continuous quality steering via health scores,
structured incident management with traceability and learning, and prevention through
merge-request risk analysis.

    The measured reduction in diagnosis time -- from 30­90 minutes to under two
minutes -- represents a significant productivity gain, reducing the cognitive load of
incident handling and freeing engineers for higher-value tasks. Entirely containerized and
deployed on Kubernetes with a Jenkins CI/CD pipeline, the solution is production-ready
and extensible.

driven architecture, LLM integration and DevOps/Cloud practices. Several future
directions emerge:
· active alerting (email, Slack, PagerDuty) on critical incidents;
· predictive ML estimating pipeline failure probability before execution;
· support for other CI/CD platforms (GitHub Actions, Azure DevOps);
· Prometheus metrics export for Grafana dashboards;
· multi-tenant isolation with per-team quotas.

Glossary

Root-cause analysis
                         The process of identifying the underlying technical cause of an
                         observed failure, as opposed to merely describing its symptoms.

Knowledge-base signature
                         A deterministic MD5 hash of an error's type and the first 100
                         characters of its normalized root cause, used as a cache key for
                         previously diagnosed solutions.

Health score  A 0­100 composite indicator computed from six weighted oper-
              ational metrics, mapped to a letter grade (A­F) per monitored
              project.

Cache hit / miss
                         In this platform's context, whether a given error signature is already
                         present in the knowledge base (hit, solution reused) or not (miss,
                         the LLM is invoked).

Webhook       An HTTP callback that an external system (here, GitLab) issues
              automatically when an event occurs (a pipeline finishing, a merge
              request opening).

BullMQ        A Redis-backed job-queue library for Node.js, used here to decouple
              webhook ingestion from the slower LLM-based analysis.

Helm chart    A packaged, parameterized description of a set of Kubernetes re-
              sources, installed and upgraded as a single versioned unit.

k3s           A certified, lightweight Kubernetes distribution suited to resource-

              constrained or edge/lab environments.

MTTR          Mean Time To Resolve: the average duration between an incident's
              detection and its resolution.
